Overview

Through an interview process and various references, this dossier explores the profession of a Research and Archives Librarian. A balance of cataloging coursework, technology skills, hands-on experience, and networking opportunities can help propel someone into this professional career. While the general job outlook is daunting, relevant hands-on experience will increase one’s likelihood of success in the industry.

Hands-on experience in the field not only provides important skills but also helps build a network within library science. Through our research for this dossier, we learned that often, who you know determines where you apply and the likelihood of landing a job. Ariel Hammond from the San Diego Natural History Museum shared that the field is small, and when applying for jobs, if the person being hired has worked for someone you trust, that already says a lot about their ability to perform well. Ariel suggests taking every opportunity you can during your MLIS degree because you never know what skill or connection could be useful later.

In researching the skills necessary for success in research librarianship, we uncovered that in today’s cultural landscape, being an information professional also means being tech-savvy. Through our interview with Ariel Hammond, we uncovered the layered nuances and uses of technology within research librarianship today. Developing foundational technology skills for a résumé is important, but, as Ariel described, a mindset of continual learning in the field is even more critical, as our digital landscape will continue to shift.

While the job market for librarians is trending downward, the demand for the skills that librarians provide is increasing. This dossier offers a unique perspective on the job market for aspiring librarians and the journey that those before us have taken to achieve their goals.

According to a recent SJSU MLIS grad and our interviewee, the job market for libraries is slim. The U.S. Bureau of Labor statistics agrees, as the market is projected grow 2% from 2024-2034, a percentage slower than the average for all occupations. It may take up to one year after graduation of consistent applications to get a job, and it likely will not be in one’s preferred field. Throughout the year, applicants may be invited to an interview from about 25% of their applications, and to a second interview from even fewer.

Our interviewee described a type of job search where the Public Library system accepts applications once every six months. Within those six months, positions will open, and one may be called for an interview. If one is not called back after six months, they reenter their resume into the pool. They describe an uncertain application system and one in which applicants far outnumber available positions, contributing to a slim job market.
Yet, while the traditional librarian job is scarce, there is a booming market for non-traditional information studies professions.

While the library system is a field with a slimming and uncertain market, it is not the only field option for MLIS graduates and librarians in training. The market for jobs requiring MLIS expertise is quite large, yet not exactly where one might expect. Jobs such as data management and curation, metadata organization, research analyst, and archiving/records management are fields calling for those with MLIS knowledge.

As digital data is created and institutions begin to accumulate that data, they must also find someone who can organize, catalogue, and communicate the data to the public, providing a growing market to MLIS graduates and those looking to work in an information field, and serve as they would if a librarian, albeit a little unconventional.

To enter a profession such as the Director of a Research Library & Archives, one should prioritize work in the field, including class projects, unpaid internships, and data management. Arial mentioned the importance of specifically taking classes that discuss cataloging, and classes that improve one’s technical coding skills, such as becoming familiar with building websites or databases.

Overall, within research librarianship, there is a growing demand for technological literacy and AI literacy as these become emerging aspects of the information profession.

AI Overview:

Currently, within research libraries, attitudes toward generative AI are wary, especially when it comes to issues of copyright and information access. Within government research libraries such as the Library of Congress, the official stance as of August 2024 is cautious about the use of generative AI for research purposes, mainly due to the sensitivity of research content and concerns over personal data and privacy. Ariel also cautioned against generative AI, which often produces a single answer in an authoritative voice, even when no real answer exists. However, in AI research, machine learning is more common, as it is used to calculate large data models and analyze information that humans could not compute in their lifetimes.

The research librarian profession is growing slowly, with in-demand skills in media and interpersonal relations. However, entry into the field is becoming increasingly difficult. Based on interviews and research, we have identified the following trends:

UNPAID AND UNDERPAID EARLY-CAREER WORK:
Unpaid internships are common and often create structural barriers to entry, particularly for individuals who must juggle other responsibilities such as jobs, classes, or family obligations. This dynamic produces clear inequities: those with greater financial resources can effectively “pay” for experience, while others cannot afford to do so. As a result, opportunities are unevenly distributed. This reflects a broader pattern in fields such as librarianship and museums, where early-career professionals often feel pressured to accept unpaid or very low-paid roles simply to get a foot in the door.

MOVING AWAY FROM UNPAID INTERNSHIPS:
When discussing the issue with Ariel from the NAT, she described her efforts to move away from the practice of unpaid internships. She emphasized refusing to normalize unpaid work as the only valid pathway into the field and instead encouraged supervisors and organizations to budget for paid roles. As an alternative, she offers internships that provide course credit and partners with fellowship and grant programs to help secure funding for interns’ labor.

PERSONAL RESEARCH:
As a research librarian, you are also encouraged to engage with your own research sounding your collection and library subject scope. When asked about personal projects, Ariel shared the level of flexibility her role allows, sharing how she is encouraged to conduct her own research on her personal interests outside of librarianship, noting how she can freely research mushrooms or community science as topics. Other institutions may require more topical research or not consider this part of the job duties.

Like Ariel, many people enter the research library field out of passion rather than financial motivation. She noted that libraries and environmental nonprofits often rely on workers’ sense of “calling,” which can make overwork feel normal. She emphasized the importance of actively resisting the urge to just help one more person before going home. Ariel shared her personal work mantra: “It’s a marathon, not a sprint,” reminding herself to pace her efforts and not try to accomplish everything at once. This serves as an important lesson for those entering the field: to take things slowly and set a healthy reminder to balance personal life alongside professional responsibilities.
