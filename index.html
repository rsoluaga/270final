According to a recent SJSU MLIS grad and our interviewee, the job market for libraries is slim. It may take up to one year after graduation of consistent applications to get a job, and it likely will not be in one’s preferred field. Throughout the year, applicants may be invited to an interview from about 25% of their applications, and to a second interview from even fewer.

Our interviewee described a type of job search where the Public Library system accepts applications once every six months. Within those six months, positions will open, and one may be called for an interview. If one is not called back after six months, they reenter their resume into the pool. They describe an uncertain application system and one in which applicants far outnumber available positions, contributing to a slim job market.
Yet, while the traditional librarian job is scarce, there is a booming market for non-traditional information studies professions.

While the library system is a field with a slimming and uncertain market, it is not the only field option for MLIS graduates and librarians in training. The market for jobs requiring MLIS expertise is quite large, yet not exactly where one might expect. Jobs such as data management and curation, metadata organization, research analyst, and archiving/records management are fields calling for those with MLIS knowledge.

As digital data is created and institutions begin to accumulate that data, they must also find someone who can organize, catalogue, and communicate the data to the public, providing a growing market to MLIS graduates and those looking to work in an information field, and serve as they would if a librarian, albeit a little unconventional.

To enter a profession such as the Director of a Research Library & Archives, one should prioritize work in the field, including class projects, unpaid internships, and data management. Arial mentioned the importance of specifically taking classes that discuss cataloging, and classes that improve one’s technical coding skills, such as becoming familiar with building websites or databases.

Typical tasks for the San Diego Natural History Museum (NAT) Director of Research Library and Archives are flexible. In smaller museum library roles, one can expertly bring a mix of service skills, technological understanding, and a willingness to adapt and learn. Ariel at the NAT shared that most of her work is on the computer and that there is very little work with physical books. She also described how often “other duties as assigned” is where most of your actual job responsibilities end up, as new problems, projects, and tasks arise as you settle into the role.

The research librarian profession is growing slowly, with in-demand skills in media and interpersonal relations. However, entry into the field is becoming increasingly difficult. Based on interviews and research, we have identified the following trends:

UNPAID AND UNDERPAID EARLY-CAREER WORK:
Unpaid internships are common and often create structural barriers to entry, particularly for individuals who must juggle other responsibilities such as jobs, classes, or family obligations. This dynamic produces clear inequities: those with greater financial resources can effectively “pay” for experience, while others cannot afford to do so. As a result, opportunities are unevenly distributed. This reflects a broader pattern in fields such as librarianship and museums, where early-career professionals often feel pressured to accept unpaid or very low-paid roles simply to get a foot in the door.

MOVING AWAY FROM UNPAID INTERNSHIPS:
When discussing the issue with Ariel from the NAT, she described her efforts to move away from the practice of unpaid internships. She emphasized refusing to normalize unpaid work as the only valid pathway into the field and instead encouraged supervisors and organizations to budget for paid roles. As an alternative, she offers internships that provide course credit and partners with fellowship and grant programs to help secure funding for interns’ labor.

PERSONAL RESEARCH: As a research librarian, you are also encouraged to engage with your own research sounding your collection and library subject scope. When asked about personal projects, Ariel shared the level of flexibility her role allows, sharing how she is encouraged to conduct her own research on her personal interests outside of librarianship, noting how she can freely research mushrooms or community science as topics. Other institutions may require more topical research or not consider this part of the job duties.

Like Ariel, many people enter the research library field out of passion rather than financial motivation. She noted that libraries and environmental nonprofits often rely on workers’ sense of “calling,” which can make overwork feel normal. She emphasized the importance of actively resisting the urge to just help one more person before going home. Ariel shared her personal work mantra: “It’s a marathon, not a sprint,” reminding herself to pace her efforts and not try to accomplish everything at once. This serves as an important lesson for those entering the field: to take things slowly and set a healthy reminder to balance personal life alongside professional responsibilities.
